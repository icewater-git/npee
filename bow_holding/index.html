While different textbooks describe slightly different approaches, the principle remains the same: all rules are derived from natural movement, and so is bow holding.

English (Steps)
  1. Thumb: Tip touches the bow stick near the frog.
  2. Middle finger: Third joint rests on the bow stick, forming a circle with the thumb.
  3. Ring finger: Third joint naturally covers the bow stick.
  4. Index finger: Second joint placed across the stick.
  5. Little finger: Tip rests gently on top of the bow stick near the end.
  6. Overall shape: The right hand forms a rounded, relaxed arch.

English

In practice, many beginners and parents neglect to correct improper bow holds. Even after some progress, the hold may still be inaccurate. Some mistakenly believe that simply â€œgrabbing the bow and producing soundâ€ is enough. This mindset is harmful: early mistakes, if not corrected, can have lasting negative consequences, potentially affecting the entire learning process.

Typical mistakes include:

  1. Incorrect thumb placement: The thumb does not touch the frog, instead pressing flat against the bow with the other fingers.
  2. Wrong thumb contact point: Thumb rests in the wrong place, not near the frog.
  3. Improper depth of hold: Fingers placed either too deep or too shallow on the bow.

Though these issues seem minor, each is critical and can severely disrupt smooth bowing.
